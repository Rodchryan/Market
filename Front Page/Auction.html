Types of Auctions

1. English Auction

  • The most common auction format where participants bid openly against one another.
  • The auctioneer raises the price until no higher bids are made.

Source: Cramton, P. (1998). "Ascending Auctions." The Journal of Economic Perspectives.

The term "auction" commonly refers to the "English style auction," characterized by ascending bids and the phrase "going, going, gone." However, this is not the only or most efficient auction method. Economic historian Ralph Cassady highlights alternative auction styles, such as the Dutch descending-bid and Japanese simultaneous-bid systems, which reflect historical and cultural influences on auction practices.

Historically, England used various methods like silent paper bids and the candle auction, as well as unique approaches like "mineing," where bidding ended when a participant shouted "Mine!" Despite these options, the English auction style became dominant due to its public, competitive, and overtly manipulated nature.

Source: muse.jhu.edu

2. Dutch Auction

  • The auctioneer starts with a high price and lowers it until a bidder accepts the current price.

Source: Kwasnica, A. M., & H. M. (2009). "The Dutch Auction: A New Approach to Selling." Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization.

In a Dutch auction, an item starts at a maximum price that decreases over time until a bid is placed. Bidders must balance the certainty of securing the item with the potential for a lower price, creating a strategic decision-making scenario.

Researchers developed a computerized Dutch auction platform to study this dynamic, conducting experiments that varied the number of items and the rate of price reduction. In their study involving 66 participants, they found no significant effects from these variations on bidding behavior. They proposed a new adaptation of prospect theory to explain group bidding behavior, which considers the trade-off between certainty and expected utility.

Source: eric.ed.gov

3. Sealed Bid Auction

  • Bidders submit written bids without knowing others' offers; the highest bid wins.

Source: Milgrom, P., & Weber, R. J. (1982). "A Theory of Auctions and Competitive Bidding." Econometrica.

A sealed-bid auction is a bidding process where all participants submit their bids simultaneously in sealed envelopes, preventing bidders from knowing each other's offers. The term "sealed bid" indicates that the bids remain confidential until they are opened on a specified date. The highest bid is typically declared the winner of the auction.

Source: investopedia.com

Key Components

Auctioneer

The individual responsible for managing the auction process and facilitating bidding.

Source: McMillan, J. (1994). "Selling Spectrum Rights." Journal of Economic Perspectives.

Bidders

Participants who place bids on items being auctioned.

Source: Vickrey, W. (1961). "Counterspeculation, Auctions, and Competitive Sealed Tenders." The Journal of Finance.

Benefits of Auctions

Market Value Determination

Auctions help establish fair market prices based on demand and competition.

Source: Ashenfelter, O., & Graddy, K. (2003). "The Demand for Expert Services: The Case of Auction Houses." The Journal of Economic Perspectives.

Access to Unique Items

Bidders can acquire rare or unique items not available through traditional retail channels.

Source: Haskel, J., & Holger, S. (2000). "The Role of Auctions in the Market for Art." The Economic Journal.

Trends in the Auction Market

Online Auctions

The rise of digital platforms has expanded reach and accessibility.

Source: Resnick, P., & Zeckhauser, R. (2002). "Trust Among Strangers in Internet Transactions: Empirical Analysis of eBay's Reputation System." The Economics of the Internet and E-commerce.

Live Streaming Auctions

Combining live events with online bidding to enhance engagement.

Source: Hsu, J., & Shiu, Y. (2021). "Live Streaming E-commerce and Its Impacts on Consumer Behavior." Journal of Business Research.

The auction market is a dynamic sector that continues to evolve with technology and consumer preferences. By understanding its various types, components, benefits, and trends, stakeholders can better navigate this marketplace.
