LAW OF ONE PRICE

Understanding the principle of price uniformity in competitive markets.

What is the Law of One Price?

The Law of One Price states that in a competitive market with no transaction costs or trade barriers, identical goods or assets will have the same price when expressed in the same currency.

How the Law of One Price Works

The principle assumes perfect information, free movement of goods, and no arbitrage opportunities. For example, if a product is cheaper in one country, traders will buy it there and sell it in another, driving prices to converge.

Benefits of the Law of One Price

  • Efficient allocation of resources
  • Encourages fair competition
  • Promotes global market integration
  • Facilitates price transparency for consumers

Challenges to the Law of One Price

  • Existence of trade barriers and tariffs
  • High transaction costs
  • Market imperfections or monopolies
  • Currency exchange rate fluctuations

Real-World Examples

A common example is the price of gold. In global markets, the price of gold per ounce is nearly identical, whether traded in London, New York, or Tokyo. Any significant price differences prompt traders to take advantage of arbitrage opportunities, bringing prices back in line.

Did You Know?

The concept of the Law of One Price underpins many financial theories, including the Purchasing Power Parity (PPP), which compares the cost of identical goods across different currencies.
